The Compound Interest Formula

This calculator uses the standard compound interest formula as taught in finance and mathematics:

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)

Where:
A = Final amount (principal + interest)
P = Principal (initial investment)
r = Annual interest rate (as a decimal, e.g. 7% = 0.07)
n = Number of times interest compounds per year
t = Time in years

Example: $10,000 at 7% compounded monthly for 10 years:
A = $10,000 × (1 + 0.07/12)^(12×10) = $20,096.61

Compounding Frequency Comparison

Frequently Asked Questions

What is compound interest?
Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal AND the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest (calculated on principal only), compound interest causes exponential growth over time — often called "interest on interest."
How much will $10,000 grow in 10 years at 7%?
At 7% annual interest compounded monthly, $10,000 grows to approximately $20,097 in 10 years — nearly doubling. This demonstrates the "Rule of 72": divide 72 by your annual rate to estimate how many years it takes to double. At 7%, 72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10.3 years.
What compounding frequency should I choose?
Choose the frequency that matches your actual account. Savings accounts typically compound daily. CDs may compound daily or monthly. Bonds typically compound semi-annually. For investment returns (stocks), annual is most common for modeling purposes. When in doubt, monthly is a reasonable default.
Does this calculator include regular deposits?
This version calculates growth on a single lump-sum investment only. It does not include recurring contributions. For calculations with regular monthly deposits (like a retirement account), you would use the future value of an annuity formula. We plan to add a savings goal calculator with recurring deposits in a future update.
What interest rate should I use for stock market returns?
The US stock market (S&P 500) has historically returned approximately 7–10% annually on average, before adjusting for inflation. After inflation, the real return is approximately 5–7%. Using 6–7% is a common conservative assumption for long-term investment projections. This is not investment advice — actual returns vary significantly year to year.
