W-2 Employee vs 1099 Contractor: The Real Hourly Difference

The converter above calculates gross pay only. But the same hourly rate on a W-2 vs a 1099 contract has very different take-home implications:

  • W-2 Employee — Your employer pays half of your Social Security and Medicare (7.65% each side). You also receive benefits like health insurance, PTO, and 401(k) matching — which can add 20–30% on top of your base salary in total compensation.
  • 1099 Contractor — You pay the full 15.3% self-employment tax on top of income tax, and no employer-provided benefits. As a rule of thumb, a 1099 contractor rate should be 20–30% higher than an equivalent W-2 salary to break even after taxes and benefits.

Example: A $60,000 W-2 salary ≈ $28.85/hr. The equivalent 1099 contractor rate to match take-home is roughly $35–$37/hr.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert my annual salary to an hourly rate?
Divide your annual salary by 2,080 (the standard number of working hours in a year: 40 hours × 52 weeks). For example, $60,000 ÷ 2,080 = $28.85 per hour. If you work a different number of hours, adjust the hours per week in the calculator above.
What is $20 an hour annually?
$20 per hour × 2,080 hours = $41,600 per year before taxes. Monthly that's approximately $3,467. Note: this doesn't account for federal, state, or local taxes, Social Security, or Medicare deductions.
Does this include taxes?
No — this converter shows gross pay only (before taxes). Your actual take-home pay depends on your federal income tax bracket, state income taxes, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), and any other deductions like 401(k) contributions or health insurance premiums. Use a payroll tax calculator for net pay estimates.
What salary do I need to earn $30 per hour?
$30/hour × 2,080 hours = $62,400 per year. If you work part-time or fewer weeks, multiply your hourly rate by your actual hours worked per year for your specific situation.
How do I compare a salary job offer to an hourly job offer?
Convert the salaried offer to hourly using this tool, then compare. But also consider: benefits (health insurance, 401k match, PTO) which can be worth $5,000–$20,000+/year, overtime potential for hourly positions, and job stability differences. Total compensation matters more than base salary alone.
