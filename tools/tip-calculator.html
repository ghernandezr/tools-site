Tip Percentage Guide

  • 10% — Below average. Use for genuinely poor service.
  • 15% — Standard. Acceptable for average service at sit-down restaurants.
  • 18% — Good. Common default for many restaurants that add gratuity automatically.
  • 20% — Great. The most common tip for good service in the US.
  • 25%+ — Exceptional service, or when you want to show extra appreciation.

Who Do You Tip?

  • Restaurant servers: 15–25% of pre-tax bill
  • Bartenders: $1–2 per drink, or 15–20%
  • Food delivery: 10–20%, minimum $3–5
  • Taxi / rideshare: 15–20%
  • Hotel housekeeping: $2–5/night
  • Hair salon: 15–20%

Tipping Outside the United States

Tipping customs vary significantly by country. Using US norms abroad can cause confusion, and in some places is actually considered rude:

  • Canada — Similar to the US: 15–20% is standard at restaurants.
  • United Kingdom — 10–12.5% is typical. Many restaurants add a "service charge" automatically — check before tipping again.
  • Europe (France, Germany, Spain) — Tipping is appreciated but not expected. Rounding up the bill or leaving small change (5–10%) is common.
  • Japan — Tipping is generally not practiced and can be considered impolite. Excellent service is a cultural standard, not something paid extra for.
  • Mexico — 10–15% is standard at restaurants. In tourist areas, 15–20% is appreciated. Always tip in local currency when possible.
  • Australia — No tipping requirement, but 10% is appreciated for good service at restaurants.

What If the Service Was Poor?

This is genuinely debated. A few honest perspectives:

  • If the issue was with the kitchen, not the server — Tip normally. Servers don't control food speed or quality in most restaurants.
  • If the server was rude or inattentive — A reduced tip (10%) is a clear signal without being punitive. Leaving nothing is rarely constructive.
  • If the experience was genuinely unacceptable — Speak to a manager before reducing or skipping a tip. It gives the restaurant a chance to respond.
  • In restaurants with auto-gratuity (18–20% added for large parties) — You can ask for it to be removed if service was genuinely poor, but this is uncommon.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I tip at a restaurant?
In the United States, tipping 18–20% is standard for good service at a sit-down restaurant. Tipping is calculated on the pre-tax subtotal in most cases, though some prefer to tip on the total including tax. For exceptional service, 25% or more is appropriate.
How do I calculate a 20% tip quickly in my head?
Move the decimal point one place left to find 10%, then double it for 20%. For a $75 bill: 10% = $7.50, so 20% = $15.00. For 15%, find 10% then add half of that: $7.50 + $3.75 = $11.25.
What is a tip on a $100 bill at 20%?
20% of $100 = $20. Your total would be $120. Split among 4 people, each person pays $30 total ($25 for food + $5 tip per person).
Do you tip on the pre-tax or post-tax amount?
Most etiquette guidelines say to tip on the pre-tax subtotal since that reflects the actual service cost. However, tipping on the total (including tax) is common for simplicity. On a $60 meal with 8% tax, the difference between tipping on $60 vs $64.80 at 20% is less than $1. Either approach is widely accepted.
Is it OK to not tip at a restaurant?
In the US, servers typically earn $2.13/hour (federal tipped minimum wage) and rely on tips for the majority of their income. Leaving no tip for standard service is generally considered poor etiquette. If the experience was problematic, a reduced tip (10%) or speaking with a manager is usually more appropriate than leaving nothing.
